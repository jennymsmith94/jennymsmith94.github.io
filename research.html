Distinct Neural Populations in the Hippocampus Shift Activity During and After Experience

How does the brain store new memories?

The hippocampus is involved in the encoding and storage of spatial and episodic memory (think- what happened, where, and the order of events). Initially, the hippocampus is largely responsible for the storage of these newly formed memories. But over time, the hippocampus “teaches” stored information to neocortical networks during offline states (quiet rest and sleep). This results in a gradual change in the neural ensembles that store the memory, shifting memory storage from hippocampal to neocortical networks. This process, known as “memory consolidation,” is thought to be driven by synaptic plasticity—a change in strength and number of neural connections—across the neocortex.

SWR-Encoded Replay Facilitates Multiple Memory Processes

So how does the hippocampus “teach” the neocortex? During offline states, the hippocampus replays experience-related neural activity patterns. These replay events are often associated with brief, high frequency oscillatory events known as Sharp Wave—Ripples (SWRs) that can recruit coordinated activity throughout the brain and the activity patterns that occur within replay have been shown to be capable of inducing synaptic plasticity. In short, SWRs are thought to be periods of time where the rest of the brain is receptive to the information being replayed by the hippocampus.

Experimentally, SWR-encoded replay has been heavily implicated memory consolidation, as inhibiting these events impairs subsequent memory performance. However, the exact role of SWRs in learning and memory processes is not fully understood. For example, SWR-encoded replay occurs during periods of time where consolidation is not expected to occur, such as brief pauses in awake behavior (grooming, consuming reward). During these awake events, replay content can often predict future behavior (I’ll explain the decoding later on), implicating these events in planning. Furthermore, when awake SWRs are disrupted during a working memory task, it impairs performance on that task. Conversely, when SWR events are artificially prolonged, it enhances performance. Because of this, it has become increasingly clear that SWR-encoded replay plays multiple roles in learning and memory across different behavioral states. To better understand how the brain may be using these events for multiple processes, I compared various features of these events before, during and after performance on a working memory task in rats.

Hippocampal Place Cells—The Brain's GPS

Before I explain my results, it will be important to understand a little bit about how the hippocampus encodes information. At the single neuron level, excitatory cells across the hippocampus respond to specific features of experience. These features are often highly associated with spatial location and are typically referred to as “Place Cells.” These cells become highly active when an animal occupies a specific location within an environment (ex: southwest corner) and are virtually silent outside of that location, which we refer to as that cell’s “place field”. Place cell activity is thought to serve as a “cognitive map,” which allows for the online representation and rapid updating of where an animal “thinks” it is—sort of like an internal GPS. Indeed, place cell activity can be decoded to yield the precise location of an animal within an environment at any given time. In my work, I used a Bayesian decoding algorithm that estimates the most probable location being represented within the brain at any given time, given the current firing rates of each place cell and that cell's known firing field. (show video)

Because hippocampal neurons exhibit tight spatial tuning, we can use spatial memory tasks to study hippocampal information processing in learning and memory. When animals repeatedly take specific paths within an environment, those paths are replayed during SWRs after experience, which we can decode based on place cell firing. Similarly, during working memory tasks, these decoded trajectories often predict future paths taken by the animals.
