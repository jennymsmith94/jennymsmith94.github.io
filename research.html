Distinct Neural Populations in the Hippocampus Shift Activity During and After Experience

How does the brain store new memories?

The Hippocampus is a region of the brain responsible for the formation and storage of new spatial and episodic memories. This region is thought to combine and integrate the elements that make up an experience—the people, objects, locations, external and internal sensations—to create a holistic view of “what's happening” and “where”. This region also links these events in sequences to allow for our perception of episodes through time, or “when” things happened and in what order.
But the hippocampus is not the only brain region involved in learning and memory. Much work over the past several decades has led to the theory of Systems Memory Consolidation, which outlines a process where the hippocampus is largely responsible for the initial encoding of experience, but over time, retrieval of that memory becomes reliant on neocortical brain regions. This shift in memory storage is known as “consolidation” and is thought to be achieved through synaptic plasticity. The hippocampus is a highly plastic brain region that can rapidly undergo synaptic changes during an experience. Thus, memory of that experience is initially stored in hippocampal ensembles. However, during post-experience rest and sleep, the hippocampus “teaches” this new information to the neocortex through “replay” of ensemble activity that represents the experience, which results in plasticity within and across neocortical ensembles.
