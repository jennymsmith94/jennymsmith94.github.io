Distinct Neural Populations in the Hippocampus Shift Activity During and After Experience

How does the brain store new memories?

The Hippocampus is a region of the brain responsible for the formation and storage of new spatial and episodic memories. This region is thought to combine and integrate the elements that make up an experience—the people, objects, locations, external and internal sensations—to create a holistic view of “what’s happening” and “where”. This region also links these events in sequences to allow for our perception of episodes through time, or “when” things happened and in what order.

The theory of Systems Memory Consolidation outlines a process where the hippocampus is largely responsible for the initial encoding of experience. Over time, the hippocampus replays experience-associated ensemble activity, which results in synaptic plasticity across neocortical circuitry so that a representation of the experience is now stored in this region. Thus, the hippocampus is the storage site of short-term memory, and neocortical regions store memory long-term.

The exact neural mechanisms that underlie hippocampal replay and facilitate information transfer across these brain regions is not well understood. Gaining insight into this process was the broad goal of my graduate research.
